# A Long Minecraft reflection

_6 December 2025_

I haven't played _Minecraft_ before. Well, I _have_, but not in the way you'd imagine - I've played its numerous clones, an open-source one even more so - Luanti, I believe it is called. And I have never been someone who played it in survival mode - for me, Minecraft was digital _Lego_ - and survival never made sense to me - my idea was that if I was placed in a world where I can do whatever I wanted, I would choose to play god. I remember this one time I was playing Minecraft (the pocket edition, the one that Mojang sold for the iPad), and I was playing it on creative mode, and my cousin - who, by the way, is seven years younger than me, never understood why I was playing it in that mode - for him and his numerous friends who played it, survival was what made sense about Minecraft - and for me, it was the opposite.

I did not even know that Minecraft had an ending - until about a year ago or so - and I was pleasantly surprised at it - and I have been toying with the idea to play the game with a friend, in a world that I would make just for the two of us, and play the game to completion. It hasn't come to that yet - given how neither one of us is in a position to purchase minecraft from Microsoft at the moment - but that is for a different conversation.

Anyway - I am writing this after what is now my longest session of playing Minecraft on survival mode - I survived a night without a house, I learnt how to craft things, I made pickaxes and swords, and fought a witch on my first night - all in all, more exciting than I had hoped. Part of the reason why I wanted to play this game (apart from the fact that I could actually sit and finish it with my friend) was so that I could use this game as a means of inquiry - to help answer a particular question that a friend and I had discussed about a month or so ago, and has been in the back of my head - but before I get to the actual question, here's what we actually have in the game which I had hoped help with my inquiry - Minecraft places you in a seemingly endless world, where you are left to your own wits - and the goal for you is to...well, survive.

Sounds simple enough, right?

Well, yeah - it is that simple of a game - except when it isn't. Minecraft has an ending. I've seen numerous people on YouTube speedrun to the ending - we're at a point where beating Minecraft (however mainstream it is, I find the idea of _beating_ Minecraft very... _weird_) is no longer a novelty - I've seen people beating the game in like two minutes or so - whether that removes the point of my inquiry or not is to be seen (spoiler alert - I think it does - but hey, I'm not using their gameplay to question life and death - I'm using _my gameplay_).

Back to where we were - Minecraft has an ending - a rather _spectacular_ one - but that is besides the point. You're placed on a vast land, left alone to fend for yourself, and you...choose. You choose, or will have to, at one point throughout the gameplay - whether to finish the game or not. You might have built entire cities by then, or you might have survived the wilderness on your own, I do not know - but there is a good chance that you might have seen many animals, plants, and the many peoples living in that world - seen them having their lives, perhaps seen them give birth or die - you would have lasted some impression in the villagers' minds - and at one point - you might choose to finish the game, and end it once and for all.

There is death in Minecraft, but it is nothing like its counterpart in real life - for one, you respawn, and you get to continue your game all over again - of course, Hardcore mode makes this interesting by giving the players permadeath - but it still isn't the same as finishing the game.

Finishing the game isn't death, of course - but I would argue that it is suicide. It certainly is not death or suicide in any of the conventional terms - but to have a choice to end things - in this case, the game, is important.

Now, here is the question - you've an entire world at your disposal - and you've enough resources to live forever, perhaps build palaces, cities, and more - perhaps you'd want to live life as a farmer, or a king - whichever it is, you've got it all. Despite all this, you also have the choice - to let go of all of it, to end the game, finishing the fate of everyone in the world that you spent your time in until now - why would you want to do it?

As I write this, it feels like a recollection of the idea of _maya_ - I do know how the game ends - and maya is presented at the end - and it _is_ one answer - but why choose to end things here, when you wouldn't do it in real life?

Well? Would you want to die right now?

I know I've felt like this before - out of curiosity, of course - but is it still worth a chance?
